NB36 Credit Policy

How We Decide Who Gets Our New Credit Card (And How Much Credit They Get)

Introduction

When you apply for the NB36 credit card, you’re basically asking us to trust you with a certain amount of our money — up to a limit. In return, you promise to pay it back. We take that trust seriously.

We want to approve as many good applications as possible, but we also need to protect the bank from fraud and customers from getting in over their heads. That’s why we have a Credit Policy. It’s a step-by-step process that checks four main things before we say “yes” or “no” — and before we decide how much credit to give you.

The Four Steps We Check

When your application comes in, it goes through these checks in order:

  1. AML Check – Are you legally allowed to have an account with us?
  2. Fraud Check – Does anything about your application look fake or suspicious?
  3. Credit Check – Have you managed credit well in the past?
  4. Income Check – Can you comfortably afford to use and pay off the card?

If you fail at any step, we stop the process right there and give a decline decision. If you pass all the way through, we combine your results into a Final Tier that tells us how much of a credit limit you should get.

Step 1 – AML (Anti–Money Laundering) Check

Before we even think about giving you credit, we have to follow strict laws to make sure you’re not involved in illegal activities. Here’s what we look for:

  • Sanctions lists – These are official lists from governments saying, “You can’t do business with this person or company.”
  • PEPs (Politically Exposed Persons) – Politicians and high-ranking officials who are considered higher risk for corruption or bribery.
  • Criminal lists – Law enforcement databases of known criminals.
  • Adverse media – News stories suggesting involvement in fraud, scams, or money laundering.

If we find your name on a serious list (like sanctions, PEP, or criminal), it’s an automatic decline — no exceptions. This is not our choice, it’s the law.

If the only thing we see is bad press (“adverse media”) but nothing legally binding, we keep going, but we make a note in your record for extra caution later. Think of this step like a bouncer at a club checking a “banned” list — if your name’s there, you don’t get in.

Step 2 – Fraud Check

Even if you’re clear legally, we need to know your application is real and not stolen, fake, or set up to trick us. We use a fraud score (0 to 100, where lower is better) plus a list of warning signs. We look for:

  • Hiding tools – VPNs, proxies, TOR browsers (all used to hide your real location).
  • Suspicious devices – Phones or computers that act like bots, or emulators pretending to be real devices.
  • Datacenter IPs – Your internet connection comes from a server farm instead of a normal home or mobile network.
  • Missing information – If your device or session data is missing, it’s suspicious.

Decisions here:

  • Decline: Your fraud score is 90 or higher, or you have multiple big red flags (like VPN + fake device + datacenter IP all together).
  • Review: Your fraud score is between 70 and 89, or you have at least one serious warning sign, or we can’t detect your device.
  • Pass: Your fraud score is under 70, and no big warning signs.

If you pass, we give you a Fraud Tier between 0 (lowest) and 7 (highest), based on your score. This step is like a cashier checking if a $100 bill is fake — we’re not judging you personally, we’re checking if the situation looks off.

Step 3 – Credit Check

If you’ve passed the first two steps, we now look at your actual financial history. This is where we check:

  1. Your credit score (V4 or FICO model) — our baseline.
  2. Serious negative events:
    • Bankruptcy in the last 7 years
    • Repossession or foreclosure in the last 3 years
    • Charge-offs (debts the lender gave up on) in the last 2 years
    • Collections over $500 in the last year
  3. Debt usage — If you’re using more than 90% of your total credit, that’s a danger sign.
  4. Late payments — Especially 90+ days late in the past year.
  5. Credit checks — If you’ve had more than 4 hard inquiries in the last 6 months, we worry you might be overextending yourself.
  6. Credit history length — Very short histories with very few accounts are riskier.
  7. Score floor — If your score is under 660, we stop here with a decline.

If you hit any big red flag above, we immediately decline (Tier 0).

If you pass, we adjust your score up or down for things like:

  • Low credit usage = good
  • Old accounts = good
  • Variety of account types = good
  • Late payments = bad
  • Too many inquiries = bad

Your final Credit Tier (0–7) comes from this adjusted score. This step is like looking at someone’s driving record before lending them a car — past behavior is a strong clue to future behavior.

Step 4 – Income Check

The last step is making sure you can afford the credit we want to give you. We check:

  • How much you actually take home each month after taxes.
  • Where we got the info — payroll records are best; if we use bank statements, we want at least 3 months of history.
  • Suspicious signs — if your income verification triggers warnings from our system.

Declines happen if:

  • You have no income.
  • Your income is less than $1,000/month.
  • You have suspicious signals AND income under $1,500/month.
  • Your bank records don’t cover enough time.

If you pass, we give you an Income Tier:

  • Tier 7: $5,000+ / month
  • Tier 6: $3,500–$4,999
  • Tier 5: $2,500–$3,499
  • Tier 4: $1,800–$2,499
  • Tier 3: $1,400–$1,799
  • Tier 2: $1,000–$1,399
  • Tier 1: $800–$999
  • Tier 0: < $800

How the Final Tier is Set

If you pass all four steps, we take the lowest tier you got from Fraud, Credit, or Income and make that your Final Tier.

Example:

  • Fraud Tier: 6
  • Credit Tier: 4
  • Income Tier: 5
  • Final Tier = 4

The Final Tier affects two things:

  1. Your approval odds — very low tiers are unlikely to be approved.
  2. Your credit limit — we multiply your monthly income by 8 to set the limit.

Example:

If your monthly income is $2,000 and you pass all checks, your limit is: $2,000 × 8 = $16,000 credit limit.
